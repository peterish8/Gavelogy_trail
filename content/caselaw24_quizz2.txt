⚖️ CQ-24-11 | Bar of Indian Lawyers v. D.K. Gandhi (2024)
📜 Passage 
In Bar of Indian Lawyers v. D.K. Gandhi (2024), the Supreme Court held that advocates’ services are not “services” under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019. It ruled that the advocate–client relationship is a fiduciary “contract of personal service”, where the client exercises control over representation, and the advocate owes duties of loyalty, confidentiality, and obedience to instructions. Such services are therefore excluded from Section 2(42) of the CPA. The Court clarified that professional misconduct by advocates must be dealt with exclusively under the Advocates Act, 1961, through the Bar Council’s disciplinary jurisdiction, and not by consumer fora. It described the legal profession as sui generis, emphasizing professional independence, fiduciary trust, and regulatory autonomy distinct from commercial service sectors.
📘 Source: Supreme Court of India, Civil Appeal No. 2646 of 2009, decided on 14 May 2024 (Justices Bela M. Trivedi & Pankaj Mithal).

 ✅ Answer: (A)
 📘 Explanation: The Court examined whether legal services are “services” under the CPA and if consumer complaints can lie against advocates regulated by the Advocates Act.

⚖️ CQ-24-12 | Bilkis Yakub Rasool v. Union of India (2024)
📜 Passage Context
In Bilkis Yakub Rasool v. Union of India (2024), the Supreme Court struck down the Gujarat Government’s remission of eleven convicts in the 2002 gang-rape and mass-murder case. The Court held that Gujarat lacked competence under Section 432 CrPC since the trial and sentencing occurred in Maharashtra, which alone was the “appropriate government.” The remission orders were declared void ab initio as they were obtained by fraud on the Court through suppression of facts in Radheshyam Shah (2022). Reaffirming Articles 14 and 21, the Court ruled that remission is a privilege, not a right, and must uphold rule of law and victims’ dignity. The judgment restores faith in constitutional justice and restricts arbitrary executive clemency.
📘 Source: Supreme Court of India, W.P. (Crl.) No. 284 of 2022, decided on 8 January 2024 (Justices B.V. Nagarathna & Ujjal Bhuyan).

⚖️ CQ-24-13 | CORE v. ECI-SPIC-SMO-MCML (2024)
📜 Passage 
In Central Organisation for Railway Electrification v. ECI-SPIC-SMO-MCML (JV) (2024 INSC 857), a five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court settled conflicting rulings on unilateral appointment of arbitrators by public bodies. By a 3 : 2 majority, the Court held that contractual clauses allowing a PSU or State entity to control appointment of arbitrators from its own panel violate Section 18 (equal treatment) and Section 12(5) (impartiality) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996, as well as Article 14 of the Constitution. Such clauses were declared unconstitutional and inconsistent with the rule of neutrality in arbitration.
📘 Source: Supreme Court of India, Constitution Bench Judgment (2024 INSC 857) – CJI D.Y. Chandrachud for the majority.

⚖️ CQ-24-14 | Child in Conflict with Law v. The State of Karnataka (2024)
📜 Passage 
In Child in Conflict with Law v. The State of Karnataka (2024), the Supreme Court clarified that the three-month limit prescribed under Section 14(3) of the Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015 for completing preliminary assessments in heinous-offence cases is directory, not mandatory. ⚖️ CQ-24-15 | Cox and Kings Ltd. v. SAP India Pvt. Ltd. (2024)
📜 Passage Context
In Cox and Kings Ltd. v. SAP India Pvt. Ltd. (2024), a five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court affirmed that the Group of Companies Doctrine (GoCD) forms part of Indian arbitration law. It held that even non-signatories can be bound by an arbitration clause if objective evidence shows their intention to be bound—through active participation in negotiation, performance, or control over the contract. The Court balanced party autonomy with commercial reality, aligning Indian jurisprudence with international standards. Consent remains central, but it may be implied from conduct, not only expressed in writing.
📘 Source: Supreme Court of India, Constitution Bench Judgment, decided on 6 December 2024 (CJI D.Y. Chandrachud; Justices Sanjiv Khanna, Surya Kant, J.B. Pardiwala & P.S. Narasimha [dissenting]).

⚖️ CQ-24-16 | Dablu Kujur v. State of Jharkhand (2024)

In Dablu Kujur v. State of Jharkhand (2024), the Supreme Court clarified the scope of Section 173(2) of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) — which defines the mandatory contents of a police report (chargesheet). ⚖️ CQ-24-18 | Dolly Rani v. Manish Kumar Chanchal (2024)
📜 Passage Context

 The Court held that solemnisation is mandatory, and registration under Section 8 only evidences a pre-existing marriage. Without the performance of customary rites, no valid marriage exists in law.
